Having worked with CUDA/Cg since its inception and worked at NVIDIA for a few years now, I was pleasantly surprised to learn about the new book The NVIDIA Way written by Tae Kim about the company and its founder-CEO. The book traverses the growth and innovation at NVIDIA since its founding in 1993 in 4 stages.

The Early Years (Pre-1993) introduce Jensen‚Äôs chilhood and upbringing as an Taiwanese immigrant child in the US and how those experiences led to his strengths in later life. Work at LSI brought him in touch with graphics hardware engineers Curtis Priem and Chris Malachowsky at Sun. Seeing an opportunity in the gaming chips market, they decided to quit their jobs and launch their new chip named NV1 and their startup as NVidia (a play on Invidia, Latin for envy).

Thanks to the technical chops of Priem and the charisma of Jensen, funding would be acquired but the young company would go through several Near-Death Experiences during 1993-2003. Priem‚Äôs choice of using quads for texture mapping in NV1 was innovative, but OpenGL/DirectX chose triangles and sounded the death knell. The quad strategy was also employed in the NV2 chip for Sega‚Äôs game console and that was abandoned. It escaped being killed by 3dfx with engineering innovations and launched Riva 128 which was a success. The graphics chips space was highly crowded and the company survived by innovating faster than its rivals.

During 2002-2013, the GPGPU usage of the parallel computing ability in graphics chips was noticed and effort was put into turning the chip into a GPU‚Äìallowing developers to program its shaders. First with Cg and later with CUDA, the parallel programming ability was generalized and supported by hardware. CUDA was promoted with researchers and universities, leading to the acceleration of every conceivable computing domain with GPUs. All of this meant that the programmable GPU was ready to spark the DL revolution when AlexNet happened. The same kind of foresight led to addition of lower precision FP, tensor cores and ray tracing support.

A final conclusion chapter goes over the unique culture that Jensen has evolved at NVIDIA. It was fun to see which of the internal workings were revealed in the book - Jensen-isms (like Speed of Light), Top 5 emails, how he communicates on emails or in meetings.

I found childhood stories of the founders, the founding and the early years of company in the graphics chips space to be interesting and enlightening. I have been working with Cg/CUDA/GPU since 2006 and the GPU acceleration of DL since the days of Caffe, so I know that history intimately and the author seems to have captured all that fairly well. Not enough insight is provided into the role of software in DL and how that is developed. The engineer in me was a tad disappointed to see that non-DL technologies like Android ARM chips, Shield, Nintendo consoles, robotics, edge inference, self-driving, Omniverse, ARM server CPU and many others that NVIDIA worked/works on did not even get a mention. Also missing is the use of GPUs in crypto mining and how that bust brought NVIDIA to the brink. Descriptions of the semiconductor industry, graphics, CUDA or deep learning in the book are good enough to understand the context of the plot, but not enough to actually understand the technology. Details of Jensen‚Äôs working style and company culture are super interesting to read, but seem to be primarily derived from its early years and interviews with early employees. This is primarily a business book, and it is a light, easy and fast read on the long pioneering journey of NVIDIA and Jensen.

Rating: 3/5
