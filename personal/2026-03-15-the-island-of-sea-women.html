The Island of Sea Women

📅 2026-Mar-15 ⬩ ✍️ Ashwin Nanjappa ⬩ 🏷️ book ⬩ 📚 Archive

Along the coast of the volcanic Korean island of Jeju lived a fascinating society of sea-diving women called the haenyeo. The Island of Sea Women follows the hard life and times of a (fictional) group of haenyeo women through the brutal Japanese occupation of Jeju, and the violence of the savage dictatorship that followed after WW2.

Child Young-Sook is inspired by her mother, their village diving collective’s leader, to become proficient in catching food from the sea floor. Growing, diving and traveling together for work, she forms a strong friendship with Mi-Ja, the orphan of Japanese collaborators. Following the arranged marriage norm of that time, Young is married to the teacher son of her mom’s friend and Mi-Ja is married to whom we discover to be a violent man. Jeju being a geographical vantage point for the Japanese forces becomes entangled during WW2 and Young loses her brothers in the war.

After the war, the villagers are further brutalized, caught in between the Communist rebels and the ruling dictatorship. The 1948 4.3 incident and the following Bukchon massacre tears Young’s life apart and the treachery of Mi-Ja forever turns Young’s heart against her. Later Young puts her life back together, South Korea sees some peace, and she is in her silver years when her life is again upturned by the arrival of Mi-Ja’s grand-daughter.

This was a beautifully crafted novel, bringing together truly moving characters and plot, a very real and painful history and a dive into a one-of-a-kind matrifocal culture. Lisa See imbues the novel with such depth and details of the haenyeo culture and the heartwrenching history of the place, that you feel like you lived through every day of Young. From start to finish, I was captivated by the characters, and the rural customs, Shamanism, astrology, all of which I thought were similar to rural Karnataka culture. The Bukchon massacre hits our characters like a typhoon and I had to walk away from the book to take a breather after that. The kind of violence that was exacted by Koreans on Koreans is truly unbelievable, especially happening under the uncaring eyes of the occupying US forces. We find some solace at the end of the story, both by the Korean goverment finally recognizing the 4.3 atrocities and by Young discovering the true reason behind Mi-Ja’s actions.

Rating: 4/5

ISBN: 9781501154850

  • Sang-Mun: husband of Mi-Ja
  • Jun-Bu: husband of Young-Sook
  • Yo-Chan: 1st child (son) of Mi-Ja
  • Min-Lee: 1st child (daughter) of Young-Sook

  • Joon-Lee: 4th child (daughter) of Young-Sook
  • Gi-Won: collective leader in Bukchon

Excerpts

The sea, it is said, is like a mother. The salt water, the pulse and surges of the current, the magnified beat of your heart, and the muffled sounds reverberating through the water together recall the womb.

How different it is with friendship. No one picks a friend for us; we come together by choice. We are not tied together through ceremony or the responsibility to create a son; we tie ourselves together through moments. The spark when we first meet. Laughter and tears shared. Secrets packed away to be treasured, hoarded, and protected. The wonder that someone can be so different from you and yet still understand your heart in a way no one else ever will.

It’s better to be born a cow than a woman.
