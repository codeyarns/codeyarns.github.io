Most people in the US have been feeling angry for a few years now. The book Angrynomics tries to explore the relation between economics and this public anger. It is presented as 5 dialogues between the 2 authors Eric Lonergan and Mark Blyth.

Dialogues 1 and 2 are kind of vague and explore different kinds of public anger. Dialogue 3 is a history of capitalism from the point of view of the authors. We begin with the relationship between capital and labor. They observe that though capitalism treats labor as just another commodity, it is actually different because labor has opinions. They define 1800s-1945 as Capitalism 1.0 where capital could move freely between nations and labor did not have a voice. After WW2, the years 1945-1980 was Capitalism 2.0 where labor unionized and got bargaining power in spreading the profits of capitalism to more of the population. In the US and most developed nations, this was the period with high taxes for the wealthy, and lifted most people into middle class, with increasing access to inexpensive housing, education and healthcare. After 1980 to the present day is Capitalism 3.0 where central banks took over and made keeping inflation low as the top priority, unions lost power and income inequality increased. In the final dialogue, the authors try to provide 3 solutions for Capitalism 4.0: creating a national wealth fund (NWF) so all citizens get a pie of the stock market growth, dual interest rates and a universal basic income based on licensing data to tech companies.

Though published in 2020, the topic of the book seems even more timely today. However, I did not really like the use of dialogue to discuss this topic. In my opinion, the first 2 dialogues examining types of anger really had no place in this book. And in the rest, there was no real direction, structure or depth, seeming more like a casual chat on a podcast. The author use the analogy of different kinds of computers (Apple vs.¬†Microsoft), software and bug fixes to explain the differences in the economic systems of US, Germany and other nations. There is a lot of repetition that people‚Äôs anger has not been addressed by centrist politicians with their small bug fix solutions, but that it requires big drastic change. So it is quite funny to see the authors themselves proposing 3 ‚Äúlets not rock the boat‚Äù bug-fix solutions at the end of the book.

Since I read the FT (where Eric is a contributor), The Economist and The Atlantic, all the anecdotes and hypotheses the authors presented were already familiar to me, thus diminishing their impact. I was hoping that there would be data, plots and statistics, but there is none of that in the book. It was also ironic to read about economic inequality and public anger from an author (Eric) who is a hedge-fund manager! Another fundamental problem with the book is that they ignore that a lot of the anger is not from economics, but has been fomented by mass media and social media algorithms in the pursuit of more eyeballs. This book was a quick read (150 pages of actual content), but an easily forgettable one, that could have really benefited from a clear structure and lots of real economic insights and depth.

Rating: 3/5
