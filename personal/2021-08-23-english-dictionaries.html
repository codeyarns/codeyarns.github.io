I love discovering the meanings of new words that I come across when reading books or reading magazines (especially The Economist). I used to just Google for the meaning, but over time I have come to love the quality of good dictionaries for this. If you are new to dictionaries, the Wikipedia page that compares English dictionaries is a good place to start off on this journey.

Below are some of the dictionaries I have used. I like trying out different dictionaries to see how they differ in the meanings, etymologies, and usage examples.

  • Oxford English Dictionary (OED): This is the ultimate reference to the words of the English language, though it focuses on the British dialect. This is a historical dictionary since it provides detailed references to the very first uses of each word. The entry for each word has meanings, pronunciation, etymology, usage and historical references. This is why each word can take a page or more, not surprising that this dictionary comes in 20 volumes! So either refer to it at a library or get the dictionary app or refer online.

  • Oxford Dictionary of English (ODE): This is the new authoritative dictionary for English. It is not a historical dictionary like OED, so the size is manageable. It is about 2100 pages in the 2010 edition.

  • Concise Oxford English Dictionary: This was earlier based on the OED, but is now based off the ODE. It has meanings, pronunciation, both British and North American usages and etymology. This is the dictionary that I have used for some time and I like it, though I would have loved something half of it‚Äôs size. It is about 1700 pages in the 2011 edition.

  • The Merriam-Webster Dictionary: This is probably the most popular reference for the American dialect of English. I used to use this while I lived in India. It is quite manageable at 700 pages for the 2005 edition.
