A Council of Dolls

📅 2024-Nov-24 ⬩ ✍️ Ashwin Nanjappa ⬩ 🏷️ book ⬩ 📚 Archive

Women from 4 generations of a Native American family are the protagonists in A Council of Dolls. The first narrator is a little girl Sissy (aka Prunella) in 1960s Chicago. She and her seemingly-alive doll Ethel watch the slow implosion of the marriage of her calm father Cornelius with her firebrand mother (also Lillian). Her mother is traumatized by her childhood forcible separation from her family to study at Catholic boarding school. Peppered with affairs and bouts of anger, her mother loses it after a grocery run with Sissy and throws herself off the roof in front of her child.

In the second part, we go back one generation to Lillian’s childhood with her 2 sisters and 2 brothers. After being separated from her parents Ina (aka Cora) and abusive Jack, she grows closer to her sister Blanche at the boarding school. In the midst of her family’s poverty, the rift with Blanche increases when Lillian wins an expensive doll (Mae). Her joy is short lived when she decides to donate the doll to a sick child Ada in their reservation and the doll is buried with Ada on her demise. Returning to school, the girls are tormented and terrorized by a Sister Francis. In the midst of the methodical cleansing of their native language in school, Blanche loses it and dies when Sister Francis forces her to ingest soap.

In the third part, we step back another generation to the childhood of Cora (Lillian’s mom). Cora and her doll Winona witness the division of her Dakota people by the creation of Indian police and the the assassination of their leader Sitting Bull.

In the final part, we return to Sissy as an adult in the 2000s. She rediscovers her childhood doll and also replicas of Mae and Winona among her mom’s possessions. The 3 dolls share their perspective of the lives of the 3 girls who played with them and try to bring some peace to Sissy about her mom’s suicide.

Since I had recently read Bad Indians, I could better empathize with the atrocious historical experiences in the book. Beneath the calm telling burns a slow and steady anger at the loss of the language, music, food and culture of the author’s Native American people. The idea of using a child and her (intellectual) doll that speaks with her is a great literary device and works well here, especially since Native Americans did believe that many things have spirits in them. I also liked the usage of the romanized Native American words and script in the book. All in all, this is an engrossing, deep and dark read by Mona Susan Power.

Rating: 4/5
