The Melancholy of Resistance reminds me of the many torturous, but pointless pursuits that people excel in to enter the Guinness World Records. Hungarian author Laszlo Krasznahorkai takes us to a small town in cold winter. Trash is piling up on the streets, stray cats abound and a Mrs.Â Plauf has returned to her home by a much-delayed night train. In the next few days, the town is beset by numerous lurkers from neighboring towns who have arrived to scalp and watch a circus that has arrived in town with the body of a giant whale. As the womenâ€™s commission head Mrs.Â Ezster jockeys for power, her home-bound husband Mr.Â Ezster and his simple-minded disciple Valuska try to feel out the changes in the town. The director of the circus and a mysterious leader The Prince lead the lurkers to ransack the town, and are put down by the army that rolls in with a tank.

The standout feature of this book is that every single page is a literal wall of text. The entire book is an endless stream of extremely long stream-of-thought sentences, with no breaks for paragraphs. If you look away for a moment, you cannot find your way back easily and if you read quickly, it feels breathless. This is probably the unique literary technique for which Lazlo got his Nobel. Unfortunately, just this literary style was not enough to justify the lack of any plot. The entire 300+ pages could be summarized in full detail in a few pages. This is because nothing really happens in the hundreds of pages, except for Mrs.Â Plaufâ€™s train journey at the start (which was somewhat interesting). The unending river of text does not really wow you with any detail, or imagination, and nor is there some twist or revelation at the end that makes the torture worth it. This is one of those rare books which I would strongly recommend avoiding.

Rating: 2/5
