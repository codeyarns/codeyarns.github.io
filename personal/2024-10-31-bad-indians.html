Bad Indians is the memoir of a half-white half-Indian (Native American) filled with musings about her family to mirror to the history and plight of California Indians. Deby (Deborah Miranda) starts off the quiet rage with the arrival of the Franciscan missionaries to California. The padres (fathers) see the soft California Indians as savages and using the power of the Spanish soldiers create missions where the Indians are enslaved together to grow food and live a strict life. Scores die due to diseases brought over from the Old World to which Indians have low resistance. After securalization, the missions disappear leaving behind Indians who have lost their stories, language, customs, religion and fashion. Further tragedy awaits them when they are hunted and sold as slaves during the period of Californiaâ€™s gold rush. In the latter half of the book which deals with the past century, the focus is on the authorâ€™s great grandparents down to her own neglective and abusive parents and their lives as Indians.

The book is a loosely ordered mosaic of biographical, historical and poetic writings. There is a quiet rage bubbling in every piece about the centuries of exploitation and how this history has ill-prepared Indians for facing the world today. The author is highly evocative in her poems, dreamy stories and tales of her family. There is a lot of repetition among the essays, like the California 4th grade school project about missions which whitewashes the atrocities of the padres and the suffering of the Indians at the missions. Unsettling to me was the domestic violence of the authorâ€™s dad and her acceptance of her dadâ€™s behavior as the product of centuries of exploitation. Some of the writing is confusing as it is difficult to discern fiction from fact. This is an intense and well researched book which could do with less detail of her parents and a cleanup of the repetition and chronological ordering.

Rating: 3/5
