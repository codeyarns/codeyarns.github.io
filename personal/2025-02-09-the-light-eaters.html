The infinity of my ignorance about plants further increased as I read The Light Eaters by Zoe Schlanger. This book looks at the spate of astonishing discoveries being made about plants in the last few decades that are showing how little we know about them.

The plants from my school science classes were essentially a green machine that grew from seeds, roots and leaves that used sunlight and water to produce our food. Scientists are now discovering that plants are able to communicate with each other, can feel touch, listen, have memory, interact actively with animals, can change themselves to mimic other plants, have a social life and even adapt to plants of their own kin. Zoe talks to the real scientific pioneers in the field of plant science, goes on scientific expeditions with them into jungles and describes the many strange and mysterious plants that are exhibiting these incredible behaviors.

The book demolished my notion that plants are boring. They are 80% by weight of the Earthâ€™s living matter and have been here before all animal life and will probably be still here after we are gone. Plants from millions of years ago, like ferns and gingko are still here with us. Ferns especially are insane beings, it was a revelation for me that they produce cork-screw sperm that swim in water to mate with female fern eggs!

We think of them as static, but they are in constant (slow) motion, probing air and soil to thrive. They certainly donâ€™t have brains, but seem to be able to sense, be aware and able to adapt to their environment. There is an entire field of phytoacoustics on how plants react to sounds. Or vernalization, where plants use their memory of winter to be able to sprout.

Plants also actively interact with animals, both being eaten and also as the predator. But mostly they coexist, sometimes even conscripting them (ants) to be their bodyguards, paying them with syrup and lodging. Probably the biggest star of the book is boquila, the chameleon vine, that can mimic the leaves of any host plant it swirls itself around! How can a plant do this unless there is some sight or genetic transfer at play?!

The popular book The Secret Life of Plants brought shame to this field, with its revelations that were non-scientific and non-reproducible. This book on the other hand is on stronger footing, with most of the findings being backed by solid science and real scientists. There are only a couple of instances where the author sympathizes with the point of view of some pseudo-scientists or philosophers, which I found unsettling. The biggest blunder in the book was the lack of pictures. I found myself looking up photos of every one of the mentioned plants and it boggles my mind why they couldnâ€™t put those in the book in the first place! Zoe is a great writer, able to wield the power of the language to draw the reader in and amaze us all over again about plants.

Rating: 4/5
