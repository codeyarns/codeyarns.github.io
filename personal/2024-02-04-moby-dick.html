Moby Dick

📅 2024-Feb-04 ⬩ ✍️ Ashwin Nanjappa ⬩ 🏷️ book ⬩ 📚 Archive

My journey with Moby Dick began 3 decades ago, on Avenue Road in Chickpete, Bangalore. It was one of those regular visits with my parents to pick up used issues of Chandamama and Champak children’s magazines that I was drawn to an illustrated pocket book of Moby Dick. Over countless re-readings I would love this tale by Herman Melville and this pocket book form factor so much that I would end up reading every one of the Moby Books Illustrated Classic Editions I could lay my hand on.

“Call me Ishmael”, that unforgettable line opens this novel, with our narrator Ishmael answering his call to be on a ship by leaving Manhattan and heading to Massachusetts. On a cold rainy night at the Spouter Inn he meets the Pacific Islander Queequeg, who would become his shipmate and harpooner later. This is one of the best bromances in literature and they end up signing up for a 3-year journey on the sperm whaling ship Pequod. Only after their journey is well underway does their broody Captain Ahab show up on deck, walking on a wooden leg and pumps up the crew to hunt and kill Moby Dick, the white sperm whale that took his leg. This crew’s journey around the world, killing sperm whales, meeting other ships that fought Moby finally ends with their own faceoff against the whale from hell.

Much like Ishmael I have felt the call to read the unabridged version in the past few years. After many such attempts, which were abandoned halfway due to the sheer size of this tome, I was finally able to see it to the end during the year end vacations. My favorite part of this book is the first act, where we see the friendship of Ishmael and Queequeg, the detailed telling of Spouter Inn, the visit to the church and our first introduction to Pequod and whaling. The middle act is especially weak, but the pace quickens in the final act with the amazing pursuit of Moby Dick. The later chapters read like plays, which I found to be not as delicious to read as Ishmael’s narration.

At 820 pages, this is a massive book, but a third of the chapters are just information about whales and whaling, which I found to be dull and skipped. Minus that stuff, this is a great tale, almost biblical in the telling. The writing is superlative, with many memorable lines worth chewing over. I read an edition which had some nice Rockwell Kent illustrations from 1958. Like most classics, this tome requires some persistence but the reader is richly rewarded with an unforgettable experience in exchange. There is simply no tale like this one and I felt truly happy reliving my childhood memories.

Rating: 3/4

ISBN: 0679600108
