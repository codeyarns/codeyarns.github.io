The Princess Diaries

A high school girl Mia (Anne Hathaway) in San Francisco discovers that she is actually a princess and heir to the throne of a small (fictional) European nation called Genovia. Her queen grandmother Clarisse (Julie Andrews) arrives in SF with her bodyguard Joe to help Anne through her high-school troubles and help her makeover into a princess. The only reason it is even watchable are the two principal women. Watched it on DVD.

Rating: 3/5

The Princess Diaries 2: Royal Engagement

Mia‚Äôs ascension to become a queen is thwarted by an ancient Genovial rule that the queen has to be married. Avoiding marriage to the young and evil Nicholas (surprise, surprise Chris Pine!), Anne tries to find love in an arranged engagement to an English Duke. Capers endure, she grows into her royal role, finally finds true love (it was Nicholas after all!), and also the guts to question the ancient rule and become queen. Just another chance to enjoy how beautiful Julie Andrews and Anne Hathaway are. Watched it on DVD.

Rating: 4/5

Premium Rush

There is no looking away when incredible bike messenger Wilee (Joseph Gordon-Levitt) is racing amidst Manhattan traffic on his fixed-gear no-brakes bike to deliver messages. The entire movie is about one delivery of his that turns out involving Chinese gangs, a corrupt cop and holds innocent lives in the balance. Some of the insane scenes looked artificial, but for the most part this is an adrenaline-pumping bike chase from start to finish. Got to wonder how they shot so many carefully choreographed heavy-traffic scenes on so many roads in Manhattan.

Rating: 4/5

Phantom Thread

Daniel Day-Lewis is Woodcock, a famous dressmaker in the 1950s, who becomes enamoured with a plain-looking lady Vicky Krieps (as Alma) and makes her his muse. Soon, his strictly controlled lifestyle grates against her personality and his spurning leads her to sicked him with a poisonous mushroom. She tends to him, he falls in love and they marry, but soon his old controlling nature riles him up.

This movie was a bit too long (needed to finish in 2 sittings), but the acting, lighting, period setting, gorgeous dressmaking scenes and cinematography were incredible. Almost the entire movie has fantastic background music that floats along with the happenings in the scene. However, I really could not understand why the two main characters acted the way they did, why these two differing personalities were attracted to each other and also the 2nd poisoning made no sense at all. It is for these reasons that I do not think I can watch it again.

Rating: 3/5

Down Periscope

Kelsey Grammar plays a Captain who has been assigned to lead a rust-bucket diesel submarine in a naval exercise against the latest tech nuclear submarines. Typical comedy with the hodge-podge submarine team finding their groove and pulling together to pull off a trick or two to win. Mindless fun movie.

Rating: 3/5

Lincoln

Based on the book Team of Rivals, this movie covers the weeks in January 1865 when Lincoln was trying to convince the House to pass his 13th Amendment (to abolish slavery) and wrap up the Civil War. Directed by Steven Spielberg and Daniel Day-Lewis is totally in character as Lincoln. We get to witness Lincoln‚Äôs intense politicking with his cabinet and balancing the relationship with his wife and sons leading up to the tense count of votes in the House. Daniel‚Äôs acting and the period setup totally immerse the viewer in the America of 1865. My only complaint is that this movie suffers from the now-common problem of a dark/black/blue color palette that is devoid of all the other colors.

Rating: 4/5

Celeste & Jesse Forever

Rashida Jones and Andy Samberg play a goofy young married couple who have been together since high school but are going through a divorce at the start of the movie. The movie deals their journey of realization that they really are not meant for each other, but are still the best friends for each other and need to remain that way. The journey is especially hard on Celeste (Rashida) who still believed that immature Andy would eventually return back to her, but is forced to realize that he is not really suitable for her. Rashida Jones is also the co-writer and apparently the plot is based personal experiences of her, the co-writer Will McCormack and friends. Rashida‚Äôs acting is great, it is Andy who probably comes off a bit too goofish for this serious role. The city of Los Angeles plays the third wheel, shot beautifully and the quirks of the people who inhabit it provide a lot of the laughs.

Rating: 4/5

Magic Mike

Channing Tatum and Matthew McConaughey bring incredible energy to their roles as male strippers. Despite that, there is only so much stripping I can handle in a movie. But, the personal aspirations and lives of the characters are actually far more interesting. I hope they built more on that in the second movie.

Rating: 3/5

Unfaithful

Suburban mom/wife Diane Lane falls for a younger French guy in the city in this erotic thriller. Indecent Proposal director Adrian Lyne is able to make every scene look gorgeous, especially upscale New York and NYC and the story unrolls like a slow car crash that you cannot look away from. Husband Richard Gere finds out eventually and his meeting the fling takes a fatal turn. Diane Lane looks and acts fantastic and carries the entire movie herself, while Richard Gere looks like wrong casting whenever his character needs to cry or be angry. Also, the movie ending with no consequences for the murderer is unsettling.

Rating: 4/5

Hope Springs

Meryl Streep and Tommy Lee Jones play empty nesters who have been married for 31 years and live like roommates now. Meryl wants to get the romance back in the relationship and she persuades the hubby to attend an intense couples therapy together, conducted by Steve Carell in a touristy Maine town. It is fantastic to watch this couple, Meryl with the expressions of a young girl still in love and Jones as a weather-beaten tax accountant. The rest of the plot is super weak though, no idea why Steve Carell was chosen, nor is it obvious how this generic counseling session is so magical.

Rating: 3/5

Side Effects

Jude Law is a psychiatrist treating a depressed and suicidal Rooney Mara with a heavily-advertized new medication when she kills her husband Channing Tatum its influence. Law‚Äôs practice and his relationship are destroyed in the wake of Rooney‚Äôs legal case before he discovers something sinister with her previous psychiatrist Catherine Zeta-Jones. Just as I was dreading having to sit through the realistically portrayed depressed life of Rooney, the movie takes a fantastic turn into the mystery genre. And after that it becomes an edge-of-the-seat thriller as you root for Law to make the impossible happen - reveal the real crime and get his life back. Jude Law is fantastic as usual, sucking you into every scene he is in and so is a middle-aged Catherine Zeta-Jones. Rooney is also incredible, being a chameleon portraying the life of her character. The movie plays at multiple levels: the ills of the US pharma industry, the over-prescribing of drugs by doctors, the complicated life of psychiatrists, the life of depression and of course the mystery. There is simply no wrong beat in this movie, it is rivetting as to be expected by director Steven Soderbergh.

Ralph Fiennes and a stellar cast of characters are involved in the drama of choosing a new pope following their secretive procedures behind closed doors. There are delicious twists and surprises and it is all highly riveting and entertaining.

Jurassic World: Dominion

There are more dinosaurs, chases through cities, a Tim Cook-lookalike villain breeding yet another new dinosaur, climate change, giant insects, Blue and the mysterious child and the original cast reunites. Watchable, but lacklustre concluding saga to the Jurassic World series.

Elio

Kicked off summer with my son by catching this on opening day at the neighborhood theater. It felt like a slightly watered down Pixar movie, with laughs, lessons and a few misty-eyed scenes for both kid and adult. The problem with Elio is that it is not memorable, the protagonist kid and the aliens he befriends are all forgettable. The movie addresses the problem of loneliness of the orphaned kid living with his aunt and I felt most drawn to it at the start of the movie when it involved the adult world. Once it went to space, it just diluted down as an experience for me.

Rating: 3/5

The Quatermass Xperiment

A sci-fi B&W B-movie from 1955 that I found to be surprisingly intriguing and entertaining. The movie begins with the rocket devised by Quatermass crash landing with a lone survivor of the 3 astronauts. The audience is kept busy with the investigations of how the other 2 might have disappeared, while the surviving astronaut escapes from the hospital and seems to be on the rampage. What seems to have happened is that some alien energy mass has invaded the astronaut‚Äôs body and is now trying to grow and reproduce. There are many cute B&W-timey horror set pieces, there is no insult to our intelligence and a great climactic set piece at Westminister Abbey. The movie is eminently watchable and it only feels dated during the few moments they need to show the monster.

Rating: 4/5

Monkey Man

Dev Patel directs and acts in this revenge action movie. Our hero Bobby is plotting his revenge against the cop and the Godman, who in his childhood, brutally killed his mom and set fire to their home in a bid to capture real estate. At night, he gets punched up in illegal fighting rings and during the day he is working his way up at the secretive Kings club, which the cop and Godman patronize. His first attempt to kill them goes horribly wrong and he is revived by a group of hijras, whose spirit enables him to return and go through Kings floor-by-floor exacting cinematic brutal bloody revenge in John Wicks style. The setting is similar to Slumdog Millionaire and the plot puts his Hanuman-like character amidst rising right-wing Hindu nationalism and characters who echo those from Ramayana. There is not a single boring moment and it is rivetting, but I am not a fan of such brutal violence on screen.

Rating: 3/5

F1

Brad Pitt stars as an aged racecar driver Sonny Hayes who returns to F1 to help a struggling team and a promising young driver Joshua Pearce. Officially blessed by F1, the movie is a shiny fun edutainment vehicle for the racing series. F1 sponsor brands festoon every inch of every visible surface and quite obviously the movie is doing a tourism infomercial for Abu Dhabi in its final race. Despite these obligations, director Joseph Kosinski keeps the plot focused on the 2 drivers, APXGP team owner, the lone female technical director and the pit crew. Even during the races, which are spread over 9 tracks, we never get to see the other racer faces and are focused on the main characters. I have never sat through a F1 race, so it was interesting to see some strategies with tires and 2 drivers, vehicles changes, all sorts of arcane realtime changes to the race if there are accidents, though it is not clear if those are all permitted in a real race. The movie is heavily cliched (does the leading man have to sleep with the only female brain on screen?), however it is thrilling and entertaining. I loved the movie score by Hans Zimmer too.

Rating: 4/5

Patton

Starting from his epic opening lines in front of a giant US flag to the end credits, I felt like I was actually watching Patton, and not George C. Scott playing the general. Patton was a highly successful general in WW2 in North Africa and the final push into Germany. This movie captures his mercurial nature, leadership style, interactions and friendships with other generals and war strategy. The writing and the 3-act plot by Francis Ford Coppola is fantastic, the vision and direction by Franklin Schaffner to show us the scale of the war exploits across multiple war locations is incredible and George C. Scott just embodies the general fully too. This movie is a feast!

Rating: 5/5

Mississippi Burning

Loosely based on the killing of civil rights activists in 1964, this movie is a whodunit set in Mississippi filled with segregation and the KKK. Great casting and acting by Gene Hackman, William Dafoe and everybody else. The shocking historical setting and plot is intriguing and the cinematography is brilliant. If the movie had not focused so much on the white characters while putting the victimized Black people in the background, it would have been even better.

Rating: 4/5

It was a delight to see the 2000-era Delhi and the delightful cast of actors, especially Naseeruddin Shah, Shefali Shah and Lillete Dubey. The plot is funny, intriguing and satisfying, especially the love between Dubey and Alice. The movie hits all the right spots.

Young Sheldon (Season 1)

I love The Big Bang Theory and was surprised to learn there is a TV series centered on Sheldon‚Äôs childhood airing since 2017! The casting of the 3 kids is perfect, especially Iain Armitage as Sheldon is a shocking mirror image of his older self. I found the characters of his mother, dad and grandmother to be just OK. Bridge of Spies

Apparently based on some true events, this is a story of the negotiations of a swap of Cold War era spies between US and Russia. Directed by Steven Spielberg, the talented insurance lawyer who does the negotiations with Russia and East Germany is Tom Hanks. First a Russian spy is caught in the US and Tom Hanks is chosen to be the defense lawyer in a cuckoo court trial. Soon a US spy plane is shot and the pilot is captured by the Russians. And also a US student from West Germany is captured by East Germany after they put up the Berlin Wall. The CIA pressures Tom Hanks to swap the Russian spy for the US pilot, but Hanks wants to also secure the release of the US student from East Germany in the same exchange. The Cold War settings are intriguing to watch, the dialogues and acting are great. However, the script does not really show how or why Hanks changed the minds of the Russians and East Germans on the swap. Another problem is that this movie follows the current plague of movies looking dull, dark and black, instead of being colorful.

Rating: 3/5

Ben-Hur

This is a classic film that had been on my list for decades now! It seems to be based on a story and hero set during the birth and rise of Jesus Christ (who is always shown from the back, without revealing his face). Judah Ben-Hur is the Jewish prince of Judea, which the new Roman tribune Messala wants to control. Turns out that Judah and Messala had been childhood friends. But that turns to hatred and vengeance when Judah refuses to turn on his people and Messala sends him to the galleys and his mom and sister to the dungeons. By a stroke of luck, Judah saves the life of a Roman naval commander and rises to be a champion charioteer in Rome. He returns back to Judea to learn that his mom and sister have died and extracts revenge on Messala by defeating him in a fantastic chariot race. He later discovers that his family is alive, but living in a leper colony. Their leprosy is cured and the family is back together when their life intersects with the sermons of Jesus and his crucification.

Though it ran to a massive 3.5 hours, I never felt the movie was too slow in any place. The plot keeps the focus on a small number of primary characters and these actors are all fantastic: Charlton Heston as Judah and Jack Hawkins as Messala. The movie has incredibly massive sets that are amazing to experience, to this day. Especially the chariot race arena is a sight to behold and no CGI can outshine it. This is truly the way a big budget movie should be made, great direction, cinematography, acting and an epic story.

Rating: 5/5
