The Princess Diaries

A high school girl Mia (Anne Hathaway) in San Francisco discovers that she is actually a princess and heir to the throne of a small (fictional) European nation called Genovia. Her queen grandmother Clarisse (Julie Andrews) arrives in SF with her bodyguard Joe to help Anne through her high-school troubles and help her makeover into a princess. The only reason it is even watchable are the two principal women. Watched it on DVD.

Rating: 3/5

The Princess Diaries 2: Royal Engagement

Mia‚Äôs ascension to become a queen is thwarted by an ancient Genovial rule that the queen has to be married. Avoiding marriage to the young and evil Nicholas (surprise, surprise Chris Pine!), Anne tries to find love in an arranged engagement to an English Duke. Capers endure, she grows into her royal role, finally finds true love (it was Nicholas after all!), and also the guts to question the ancient rule and become queen. Just another chance to enjoy how beautiful Julie Andrews and Anne Hathaway are. Watched it on DVD.

Rating: 4/5

Premium Rush

There is no looking away when incredible bike messenger Wilee (Joseph Gordon-Levitt) is racing amidst Manhattan traffic on his fixed-gear no-brakes bike to deliver messages. The entire movie is about one delivery of his that turns out involving Chinese gangs, a corrupt cop and holds innocent lives in the balance. Some of the insane scenes looked artificial, but for the most part this is an adrenaline-pumping bike chase from start to finish. Got to wonder how they shot so many carefully choreographed heavy-traffic scenes on so many roads in Manhattan.

Rating: 4/5

Phantom Thread

Daniel Day-Lewis is Woodcock, a famous dressmaker in the 1950s, who becomes enamoured with a plain-looking lady Vicky Krieps (as Alma) and makes her his muse. Soon, his strictly controlled lifestyle grates against her personality and his spurning leads her to sicked him with a poisonous mushroom. She tends to him, he falls in love and they marry, but soon his old controlling nature riles him up.

This movie was a bit too long (needed to finish in 2 sittings), but the acting, lighting, period setting, gorgeous dressmaking scenes and cinematography were incredible. Almost the entire movie has fantastic background music that floats along with the happenings in the scene. However, I really could not understand why the two main characters acted the way they did, why these two differing personalities were attracted to each other and also the 2nd poisoning made no sense at all. It is for these reasons that I do not think I can watch it again.

Rating: 3/5

Down Periscope

Kelsey Grammar plays a Captain who has been assigned to lead a rust-bucket diesel submarine in a naval exercise against the latest tech nuclear submarines. Typical comedy with the hodge-podge submarine team finding their groove and pulling together to pull off a trick or two to win. Mindless fun movie.

Rating: 3/5

Lincoln

Based on the book Team of Rivals, this movie covers the weeks in January 1865 when Lincoln was trying to convince the House to pass his 13th Amendment (to abolish slavery) and wrap up the Civil War. Directed by Steven Spielberg and Daniel Day-Lewis is totally in character as Lincoln. We get to witness Lincoln‚Äôs intense politicking with his cabinet and balancing the relationship with his wife and sons leading up to the tense count of votes in the House. Daniel‚Äôs acting and the period setup totally immerse the viewer in the America of 1865. My only complaint is that this movie suffers from the now-common problem of a dark/black/blue color palette that is devoid of all the other colors.

Rating: 4/5

Celeste & Jesse Forever

Rashida Jones and Andy Samberg play a goofy young married couple who have been together since high school but are going through a divorce at the start of the movie. The movie deals their journey of realization that they really are not meant for each other, but are still the best friends for each other and need to remain that way. The journey is especially hard on Celeste (Rashida) who still believed that immature Andy would eventually return back to her, but is forced to realize that he is not really suitable for her. Rashida Jones is also the co-writer and apparently the plot is based personal experiences of her, the co-writer Will McCormack and friends. Rashida‚Äôs acting is great, it is Andy who probably comes off a bit too goofish for this serious role. The city of Los Angeles plays the third wheel, shot beautifully and the quirks of the people who inhabit it provide a lot of the laughs.

Rating: 4/5

Magic Mike

Channing Tatum and Matthew McConaughey bring incredible energy to their roles as male strippers. Despite that, there is only so much stripping I can handle in a movie. But, the personal aspirations and lives of the characters are actually far more interesting. I hope they built more on that in the second movie.

Rating: 3/5
